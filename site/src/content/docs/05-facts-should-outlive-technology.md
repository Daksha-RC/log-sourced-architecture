Technology changes.

This is one of the few constants in software engineering.

Applications are rewritten.

Databases are replaced.

Cloud providers change.

Analytics platforms evolve.

AI models come and go.

Yet despite this continuous change, organizations often possess information that remains valuable for decades.

A financial transaction, a medical observation, a policy decision, a supply chain event, or a customer interaction may outlive multiple generations of technology.

This raises an important question:

**Why do we so often bind long-lived facts to short-lived technologies?**

## The Migration Cycle

Every organization eventually encounters a migration.

A database reaches its limits.

A vendor increases prices.

A cloud strategy changes.

A new analytical platform emerges.

An AI framework becomes obsolete.

What follows is often a costly effort to move information from one technology stack to another.

The larger the organization, the larger the migration.

In many cases, the challenge is not the technology itself.

The challenge is disentangling valuable information from the systems that currently store it.

Organizations discover that they are not merely migrating software.

They are migrating years or decades of accumulated knowledge.

## Facts Have a Different Lifespan

Most technologies have a lifespan measured in years.

Facts often have a lifespan measured in decades.

A bank may retain transaction histories for generations.

Manufacturers may maintain records throughout the life of a product.

Governments preserve records indefinitely.

Healthcare systems rely on historical information spanning decades.

The facts remain relevant even as every surrounding technology changes.

This mismatch in lifespan creates a fundamental architectural challenge.

When facts become tightly coupled to a particular technology, every future change becomes more difficult.

## Vendor Neutrality Is Not Enough

Organizations often discuss avoiding vendor lock-in.

While important, vendor neutrality addresses only part of the problem.

Technology evolves at many layers.

Storage engines change.

Data formats change.

Analytics platforms change.

Search technologies change.

AI ecosystems change.

Even open technologies eventually become legacy technologies.

The goal should not simply be freedom from a specific vendor.

The goal should be preserving facts independently of the technologies used to process them.

## Technology Should Be Replaceable

Architectures should allow organizations to adopt better technologies without rewriting history.

A new database should not require redefining the past.

A new analytics engine should not require rebuilding organizational knowledge.

A new AI platform should not require rediscovering facts that already exist.

Technologies should be replaceable.

Facts should remain stable.

When facts become the enduring asset, technological evolution becomes significantly easier.

Organizations gain the freedom to adopt innovation without sacrificing continuity.

## A Foundation for Future Systems

Perhaps the most important consumers of facts have not been built yet.

Future analytical systems will need them.

Future AI models will need them.

Future applications will need them.

Future technologies that do not yet exist will need them.

Architectures that preserve facts independently of current technologies provide a bridge between generations of systems.

They allow organizations to evolve without losing continuity.

## Facts Are Permanent. Implementations Are Temporary.

Software engineering often focuses on selecting technologies.

Which database?

Which cloud provider?

Which analytical platform?

Which AI model?

These decisions matter.

But history suggests that every technology choice is temporary.

The more important question is whether the underlying facts can survive those choices.

Technology should evolve.

Organizations should innovate.

Platforms should be replaced when better alternatives emerge.

Through all of this, the facts should remain intact.

Because while technologies are temporary, organizational knowledge is not.

## Looking Ahead

Technology changes are not the only challenge long-lived systems face.

Knowledge itself evolves.

New facts emerge.

Understanding improves.

Corrections become necessary.

The question is not how to preserve a snapshot of the past.

The question is how to preserve history while allowing knowledge to grow.

In the next article, we'll explore why immutable facts matter and why preserving history may be one of the most powerful capabilities a software system can possess.
