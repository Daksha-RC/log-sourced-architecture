## 1. Foundational Concepts

### 1.1 What exactly is a "fact" in LSA?

A **fact** is an immutable, timestamped statement that something happened.
It has four essential properties:

- **Atomic** — represents one observation, not a batch.
- **Immutable** — once written, it is never updated or deleted.
- **Self-describing** — carries its own schema version and semantic identity.
- **Temporally anchored** — records *when* it occurred and *when* it was observed.

**Example**

```json
{
  "type": "OrderPlaced",
  "orderId": 9182,
  "customerId": 314,
  "amount": 49.90,
  "occurredAt": "2026-06-22T08:14:02Z"
}
````

A **state** like `Order.status = "Shipped"` is *not* a fact — it is a *projection*
derived from one or more facts (`OrderPlaced`, `OrderPacked`, `OrderShipped`).

***

### 1.2 Why is it called *Log-Sourced* and not *Event-Sourced*?

Both rely on append-only sequences, but the **intent differs**:

| Dimension        | Event Sourcing                  | Log-Sourced Architecture                      |
| ---------------- | ------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------- |
| Scope            | Per-aggregate (bounded context) | Organization-wide source of truth             |
| Primary consumer | The owning service              | Any consumer — services, analytics, AI, audit |
| Replay purpose   | Rebuild aggregate state         | Reconstruct *any* view, including new ones    |
| Schema ownership | Domain service                  | Shared fact catalog                           |
| Coupling to DDD  | Tight                           | Optional                                      |

LSA treats the **log itself** — not a service's aggregate — as the architectural center of gravity.

> 💡 Think of Event Sourcing as a *pattern within a service*, and Log-Sourced Architecture
> as a *pattern for an organization*.

***

### 1.3 Is LSA the same as Kappa Architecture or Data Mesh?

No, though it shares DNA with both.

* **Kappa** is a *data-processing topology* (one stream, no batch layer). LSA is broader —
  it covers governance, AI grounding, explainability, and portability.
* **Data Mesh** distributes ownership of *data products*. LSA can run **inside** a Data Mesh:
  each domain publishes its facts as a product, and the log is the contract.
* **Lakehouse** unifies analytics storage. LSA is upstream of the lakehouse — the lakehouse
  becomes one of many *consumers* of the facts.

## 2. Architecture & Implementation

### 2.1 Won't an immutable log grow forever? Isn't that expensive?

Storage cost is rarely the bottleneck — object storage is \~$0.02/GB/month.
What matters is **structure**:

* **Tiered storage** — hot facts in the broker, cold facts in object storage (Kafka Tiered Storage, Pulsar Offloaders).
* **Compaction** — retain the *latest fact per key* where full history isn't required.
* **Snapshots** — periodic projections allow consumers to skip log scans.
* **Archival** — older facts move to cheaper, queryable formats (Parquet on S3).

The cost of *losing* history is typically far greater than the cost of *keeping* it.

***

### 2.2 How does LSA handle schema evolution?

Schemas evolve; facts don't.

* Use **forward- and backward-compatible** formats (Avro, Protobuf).
* Every fact carries its **schema version**.
* Projections are **versioned**, not migrated — a v2 projection runs alongside v1 until consumers cut over.
* Breaking changes are introduced as **new fact types**, not mutations of old ones.

***

### 2.3 What about GDPR, "right to be forgotten," and PII?

This is the hardest practical question for any immutable system. Common patterns:

* **Crypto-shredding** — PII is encrypted with a per-subject key; deleting the key renders the
  data unreadable while preserving the structural fact.
* **Tombstone facts** — a `SubjectErased` fact instructs all projections to drop derived data.
* **PII segregation** — sensitive attributes live in a separate, deletable store; the log
  references them by ID.

> ⚠️ LSA does not exempt you from privacy law. It changes *how* you comply: by adding facts,
> not by mutating them.

***

## 3. Adoption & Migration

### 3.1 Do we have to rewrite everything?

No. LSA is adopted **incrementally**:

1. **Identify a high-value domain** — typically one suffering from sync pain or audit gaps.
2. **Introduce a fact log alongside** the existing database (dual-write or CDC).
3. **Build one projection** — usually a read model that consumers prefer.
4. **Retire the legacy read path** once the projection earns trust.
5. **Repeat** per domain.

The legacy database does not disappear overnight. It is gradually **demoted from source of
truth to one more projection**.

***

### 3.2 When is LSA *not* the right choice?

* Small, single-service applications where state-centric design is sufficient.
* Highly regulated workloads where **hard deletion**, not logical erasure, is mandated and
  crypto-shredding is not accepted by counsel.
* Teams without the operational maturity to run a log substrate as a Tier-1 service.

LSA is an investment. It pays off when **history, explainability, or AI grounding** are
strategic — not when they are nice-to-have.

***

### 3.3 What does success look like?

* Engineers stop writing synchronization pipelines.
* "Why is this number wrong?" becomes a query, not an investigation.
* New consumers — analytics, AI, partners — are added without touching producers.
* Audit and compliance reports become byproducts of the log, not bespoke projects.

***

## 4. Glossary

| Term                 | Definition                                                           |
| -------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Fact**             | An immutable, timestamped statement that something happened.         |
| **Log**              | An ordered, append-only sequence of facts; the system of record.     |
| **Projection**       | A derived view of the log optimized for a specific consumer.         |
| **State**            | The current value of a projection — always derivable, never primary. |
| **Reasoning**        | The explicit rules or models that transform facts into decisions.    |
| **Replay**           | Reprocessing the log to rebuild a projection or audit a decision.    |
| **Compaction**       | Retaining only the latest fact per key to bound storage.             |
| **Materialization**  | The act of computing and storing a projection.                       |
| **Crypto-shredding** | Erasing data by destroying its decryption key.                       |
